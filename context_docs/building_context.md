## General District Context

### Construction Era and Materials

The Montpelier Historic District's commercial core along State Street, Main Street, Elm Street, and Baldwin Street developed predominantly between roughly 1840 and 1925, with a smaller cluster of mid-20th-century infill (1949–1994) and one late-20th-century church (1972). Residential streets such as Baldwin Street, Bailey Avenue, and the upper sections of Elm and Langdon Streets are dominated by **wood-frame construction**, typically 2 to 2.5 stories, in styles spanning Greek Revival (c.1830s–1850s), Italianate (c.1850s–1880s), Queen Anne (c.1880s–1900s), Shingle Style, Colonial Revival, and Dutch Colonial Revival (c.1900–1920s). These wood-frame houses generally sit on raised foundations (stone or brick/concrete) with at least a partial basement or crawl space, common in Vermont's hilly terrain and high water table near the Winooski River and its North Branch.

The institutional and commercial core of State Street is dominated by **brick, granite, and marble masonry construction**, often 3 to 5 stories, including high-style examples of Second Empire/French Second Empire (e.g., the 1870 Vermont Mutual Fire Insurance building), Romanesque (the 1891 National Life Insurance Co. building, brick and sandstone), Neo-Classical Revival (the 1916 Supreme Court/State Library and the 1921–22 National Life Insurance office, both granite), Greek Revival/Renaissance Revival (the 1836/1859 Vermont State House, granite, a National Historic Landmark), and Art Deco/Modernist (the 1949 DMV building, marble/granite/concrete). A handful of mid- to late-20th-century non-contributing buildings (brick veneer or curtain-wall construction, c.1958–1994) were inserted into the streetscape, generally replacing earlier 19th-century buildings lost to fire, demolition, or urban renewal. Several buildings show evidence of major alteration or partial reconstruction after historic fires (e.g., the Pavilion Hotel, rebuilt 1971 following the 1970s-era reconstruction of the 1876 original).

Foundations throughout the district vary by construction era and lot topography: earlier 19th-century wood-frame and masonry buildings commonly have rubble stone or dressed granite foundations with masonry or dirt-floor basements, while early-to-mid 20th-century buildings (and renovations) more often have poured concrete foundations and finished basements. Many State Street buildings back directly onto the North Branch of the Winooski River or sit only one parcel removed from the riverbank, placing their rear facades, foundations, and below-grade mechanical spaces at elevated flood risk relative to their street-facing facades.

### Masonry Flood Damage Principles

Floodwater inflicts damage on historic masonry through several interacting mechanisms. **Hydrostatic pressure** (lateral pressure proportional to the square of water depth, f = γd²/2) builds against basement walls and foundations, and if not relieved by flooding the interior (to equalize pressure) or by adequate drainage, can cause wall bowing, cracking, or collapse — particularly in unreinforced masonry walls lacking a continuous load path between floors, walls, and foundation. **Hydrodynamic forces** from moving floodwater (fdyn = ½ρCdV²A) add a velocity-dependent lateral load, which is significant in Montpelier given the Winooski River's documented high flow velocities during the July 2023 event. **Debris impact loads** — from floating logs, vehicles, dumpsters, and building fragments carried by floodwaters — can cause concentrated, sudden damage to masonry walls, window and door openings, and porches, especially on upstream (riverward) facades.

**Scour** (local scour at foundation corners, contraction scour where flow is constricted by bridges/buildings, and channel degradation scour) can undermine shallow foundations, particularly for buildings close to the riverbank or built on grade near bridge approaches (e.g., near the Taylor Street, Langdon Street, and railroad bridges). At the material level, prolonged saturation significantly degrades the unconfined compressive strength (UCS) of historic brick, lime mortar, and stone — reductions of 50–70% have been documented — making walls more vulnerable to subsequent loading, freeze-thaw spalling, and salt crystallization (efflorescence/subflorescence) as floodwater evaporates from saturated masonry. Out-of-plane (OOP) wall failure, where a wall bulges and collapses perpendicular to its plane due to lost lateral support (often from a failed floor diaphragm or removed cross-wall), is one of the most common severe failure modes in flood-damaged unreinforced masonry, and is distinct from in-plane racking/shear damage. Buildings with a continuous, well-connected load path between roof, floor diaphragms, walls, and foundation perform substantially better; this includes that masonry pier or wall connections to floor framing remain intact even after water has saturated the lower courses. Damage assessment and mitigation strategies described in the literature include wet floodproofing (allowing controlled water entry to equalize pressure, used for less valuable spaces such as basements/crawlspaces) versus dry floodproofing (sealing the building envelope, typically only effective for shallow flood depths and structurally robust walls), and the use of fragility curves relating flood depth/duration/velocity to probability of given damage states — useful for triaging the relative vulnerability of the buildings in this dataset based on their construction type, foundation type, and proximity to the river/HWM locations.

### High-Water Mark (HWM) Data Notes

The HWM dataset used here is the USGS data release "High-Water Mark Data from the July 2023 Flood in Vermont" (Olson, LeNoir, Kalmon, and Ahearn, 2025; DOI: https://doi.org/10.5066/P14XBBAT), filtered to the Montpelier area (58 records along the North Branch Winooski River, the main-stem Winooski River, and Stevens Branch). Each record reports a "Height Above Ground" value (in feet) — the vertical height of the flood mark above the ground surface at the mark's location, NOT necessarily the flood depth at a specific building's foundation or first floor — along with a "Surveyed HWM Elevation" in feet (presumably NAVD88 datum) and a qualitative "HWM Quality" rating (Excellent ±0.05 ft to Poor ±0.40 ft, or "Unknown/Historical" for marks of uncertain age/origin). HWM types include seed lines (debris/sediment lines left by receding water), mud lines, stain lines, and debris marks (isolated deposits, generally lower-confidence indicators of peak elevation). When using these values for building-specific flood depth estimates, note that the height-above-ground figure reflects the local ground elevation at the HWM location, which may differ from the ground elevation at a given building's foundation, especially where buildings are set back from the river or on sloped lots.
